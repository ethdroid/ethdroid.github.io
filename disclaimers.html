Market Indices & Benchmarks

References to market or composite indices, benchmarks or other measures of relative market performance over a specified period of time (such as ETFs) are provided for your information only. Indices are not selected to represent an appropriate benchmark with which to compare an investor’s performance and do not imply that portfolios managed by the Advisor will achieve returns, volatility or other results similar to an index. Their use is merely intended to allow for comparison of the investor’s performance to that of certain well-known and widely recognized indices. The S&P 500 Index is a broadly-based index of the common stock prices of 500 large cap U.S. companies that include the reinvestment of dividends. The 10-year Treasury Bond Index is an index that measures the performance of U.S. Treasury securities with a remaining maturity of at least seven years and less than 10 years. The use of the S&P 500 and 10-year Treasury Bond Index is merely intended to reflect traditional stock and bond strategies, so they should not be considered performance benchmarks. The Dow Jones Industrial Average is an index of 30 stocks traded in the U.S. and designed to gauge the performance of the industrial sector of the American stock market. The FTSE 100 is the most well-known index, which is comprised of blue-chip stocks listed on the London Stock Exchange. The NASDAQ Composite Index measures all NASDAQ domestic- and international based common type stocks listed on the NASDAQ Stock Market. The Thomson Reuters/Jefferies CRB Index is an index of 19 commodities designed to provide an accurate representation of broad commodity price trends. ETFs, or exchange-traded funds, are funds that track indices so, when you buy shares of an ETF you are buying shares of a portfolio that tracks the yield and return of its corresponding index. A major difference between ETFs and other types of index funds is that ETFs do not try to outperform their index, but simply replicate its performance. For example, SPY is one of three ETFs offering exposure to the S&P 500; DIA is the only ETF linked to the Dow Jones Industrial Average; and IWM is linked to a broad-based index of small cap U.S. equities. EFA offers exposure to developed markets outside the U.S., including Europe, Japan, and Australia. EEM offers easy access to hundreds of emerging markets stocks. AGG focuses on high quality U.S. debt, including Treasuries and investment grade corporate bonds. IAU is one of four ETFs that invest in physical gold and is one of the largest holders of gold bullion in the world.
